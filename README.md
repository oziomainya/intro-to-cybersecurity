## Part 1: The Cybersecurity Landscape

### What Is Cybersecurity?
Cybersecurity is the practice of protecting systems, networks, and programs
from digital attacks that aim to access, change, or destroy sensitive information,
extort money, or disrupt operations.

### The Scale of the Problem
The 2021 World Economic Forum Global Risks Report listed cybersecurity failure
among the top five global risks. Organisations of every size are targeted, from
multinationals to small businesses, schools, and hospitals. No sector is immune.

### Current Threat Trends
Key trends include ransomware targeting critical infrastructure, nation-state
sponsored espionage, supply chain attacks on trusted software vendors, and the
weaponisation of AI by threat actors. The shift to remote work has significantly
expanded the attack surface.

### The Skills Gap
Millions of cybersecurity positions worldwide remain unfilled due to a critical
skills gap, creating both a risk for organisations and an opportunity for
professionals entering the field.

## Part 2: Attacks, Concepts and Techniques

### Threat Actors
Threat actors range from opportunistic script kiddies, to organised criminal
groups running ransomware-as-a-service, to state-sponsored APT groups.
Motivations include financial gain, espionage, hacktivism, and sabotage.

### Malware Types

| Type | Description |
|---|---|
| Virus | Attaches to files and spreads on execution |
| Worm | Self-replicates across networks without user interaction |
| Trojan | Disguises as legitimate software |
| Ransomware | Encrypts data and demands payment for decryption |
| Spyware | Silently collects user information |
| Adware | Delivers unwanted ads and tracks browsing |

### Social Engineering
Phishing, spear phishing, vishing, pretexting, and baiting all exploit human
psychology; trust, urgency, and fear, rather than technical vulnerabilities.
The common thread is manipulation.

### Network Attacks
DoS/DDoS floods targets with traffic. Man-in-the-Middle intercepts communications.
Packet sniffing captures traffic in transit. IP spoofing forges packet headers.
Replay attacks reuse captured authentication data.

### Application Attacks
SQL injection, cross-site scripting (XSS), buffer overflow, and zero-day exploits
are among the most common. Zero-days are particularly dangerous because no patch exists
at the time of the attack.

## Part 3: Protecting Your Data and Privacy

### Authentication and Passwords
Strong passwords should be long (12+ characters), complex, and unique per account.
Multi-factor authentication (MFA) significantly reduces account compromise risk
even when passwords are stolen. Enable MFA everywhere it is supported.

### Encryption
Encryption protects data at rest, in transit, and in use.
HTTPS, end-to-end encryption in messaging, and full disk encryption are
the three most important practical applications.

### The 3-2-1 Backup Rule
Keep at least three copies of data, on two different storage media,
with one copy stored offsite or in the cloud. Test backups regularly.
This is the single most effective defence against ransomware.

### Safe Online Practices
- Keep systems and software updated
- Use a VPN on public Wi-Fi
- Be cautious with email links and attachments
- Review app permissions regularly
- Monitor account activity for unauthorised access

## Part 4: Protecting the Organisation

### The CIA Triad

| Property | Definition | Example violation |
|---|---|---|
| Confidentiality | Data only accessible to authorised parties | Data breach |
| Integrity | Data is accurate and untampered | Data manipulation |
| Availability | Systems accessible when needed | Ransomware/DoS |

### Security Frameworks
- **NIST Cybersecurity Framework** : Identify, Protect, Detect, Respond, Recover
- **ISO/IEC 27001** : International standard for information security management

### Access Control and Least Privilege
The principle of least privilege limits users and systems to only the access
they need. Role-based access control (RBAC) assigns permissions by job role.
These controls reduce the blast radius of a breach.

### Incident Response Phases
Preparation > Identification > Containment > Eradication > Recovery > Lessons Learned

### Legal Obligations
GDPR requires breach notification within 72 hours. The CFAA criminalises
unauthorised computer access. Cybersecurity professionals must operate
within legal and ethical boundaries at all times.

## Reflections

**1. Cybersecurity is everyone's responsibility**

Social engineering attacks target people, not systems. Understanding how
attackers exploit psychology is just as important as technical knowledge.

**2. The threat landscape is constantly evolving**

Attack techniques that were cutting-edge five years ago are now automated
and accessible to low-skill attackers. Staying current is a core part of the job.

**3. Frameworks give structure to what feels overwhelming**

The NIST Cybersecurity Framework and the CIA triad provide a structured way
to think about any security problem. They appear consistently across the industry.

**4. This is the foundation everything else builds on**

Introduction to Cybersecurity provides the vocabulary and mental models for
everything that follows in the career path.
